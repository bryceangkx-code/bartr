Brand partnerships can be exciting — free products, paid collaborations, and the chance to work with labels you genuinely admire. But not every deal is a good deal, and saying yes to the wrong one can cost you more than it gains: lost credibility with your audience, unpaid work, or content you regret putting your name on.

Whether you're a Singapore-based lifestyle creator receiving your first product gifting request or a Jakarta food blogger fielding your fifth brand email this week, these are the five things worth knowing before you agree to anything.

## 1. Understand Exactly What's Being Asked of You

The most common source of frustration in brand partnerships isn't the product or the compensation — it's misaligned expectations. A brand assumes you'll post three times; you thought once was fine. They wanted a Reel; you made a Story. They expected usage rights; you didn't know that was part of the deal.

Before agreeing to anything, get clear answers to these questions:

- **What deliverables are required?** Number of posts, formats (Reel, Story, static, TikTok), and which platforms.
- **What's the timeline?** When does content need to go live? How much time do you have to receive the product, use it, and create content?
- **What's the approval process?** Does the brand want to review content before it goes live? How many rounds of revision are included?
- **Are there usage rights involved?** Will the brand repost your content, use it in ads, or feature it on their website?

If the brand can't answer these questions clearly in their initial outreach, ask. A legitimate brand with professional marketing expectations will have these details ready. Vagueness from the brand's side is a yellow flag.

### What's Reasonable to Expect

For barter (product-only) arrangements, a reasonable deliverable scope is typically one to two posts or one short-form video per product received. If a brand is asking for five pieces of content in exchange for a $30 item, that's not a balanced exchange — it's unpaid labor dressed up as a partnership.

For paid collaborations, the deliverable scope should be proportional to the fee. One feed post and two Stories for a few hundred dollars is common at the micro tier. Be wary of brands that ask for extensive exclusivity (more on that below) or multiple platform deliverables for rates that don't reflect the actual work involved.

## 2. Know Your Content Rights — and Protect Them

When you create content, you own it. That's the default legal position in most jurisdictions, including Singapore. But many brands include usage rights clauses in their briefs or contracts that transfer or license those rights — sometimes far more broadly than creators realize.

Common usage rights asks include:

- **Repurposing on brand social channels** (usually fair for both paid and barter deals)
- **Use in paid advertising** (this has real monetary value and should be compensated separately if you're not being paid for the original content)
- **Exclusivity clauses** (preventing you from working with competing brands for a specified period)
- **Whitelisting** (allowing the brand to run paid ads from your account directly)

For barter deals, it's reasonable to allow the brand to repost your content with credit. It's not reasonable for them to run paid ads using your content and likeness without additional compensation.

Read any written brief or contract carefully. If it mentions "all platforms," "in perpetuity," or "worldwide rights," you're being asked to give up significant value. You can negotiate these terms — good brands expect it.

## 3. Evaluate the Brand, Not Just the Product

A free product is only as valuable as the brand behind it. Before agreeing to work with any brand, do a basic vetting:

**Check their existing presence.** What does their social content look like? How do they respond to customer comments? Do they seem like a brand your audience would trust?

**Research the product.** Is it something you'd genuinely use? If you're a Singapore wellness creator with a health-conscious audience and a brand is asking you to promote something you have doubts about, say no. Your audience trusts you partly because they believe you mean what you say.

**Look for any red flags.** A quick search for the brand name plus "reviews" or "complaints" can surface patterns you'd want to know about before putting your name next to theirs.

**Consider brand-audience fit.** Even if you like the product, ask whether your audience will find it relevant. A home decor creator with a strong following of Singapore HDB dwellers might not be the right fit for a luxury outdoor furniture brand. A mismatched partnership can confuse your audience and underperform regardless of effort.

## 4. Disclosure Is Not Optional

In Singapore, the Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) and the Infocomm Media Development Authority (IMDA) guidelines are clear: if you receive compensation of any kind — including free products — in exchange for content featuring a brand, that relationship must be disclosed to your audience.

This applies even when no money changes hands. Receiving a product and posting about it without disclosure is considered misleading advertising.

The standard approaches:

- On Instagram: "#gifted", "#ad", "#sponsored", or "#collab" in the caption or visible in the video
- On TikTok: the paid partnership label, or a clear verbal or text disclosure in the video
- On YouTube: verbal disclosure near the start of the video and in the description

Some creators worry that disclosure will hurt engagement or make their audience skeptical. Research generally suggests the opposite: audiences respect transparency and are more likely to trust creators who are upfront about partnerships than those who hide them. Your long-term credibility is worth more than a momentary awkwardness about labeling a post.

## 5. Watch for Red Flags That Signal a Bad Deal

Not every brand outreach is legitimate, and even legitimate brands sometimes propose arrangements that aren't worth your time. Here are the warning signs to watch for:

**"Exposure" as payment.** Any brand that frames the opportunity primarily in terms of "growing your audience" or "exposure to our followers" without offering meaningful compensation (cash or genuine product value) is likely not respecting your work.

**Vague or changing briefs.** A brand that can't tell you clearly what they need, or that keeps expanding the scope after you've agreed, is going to be a difficult working relationship.

**Pressure tactics.** "We need a decision today" or "We only have one spot left" — these are pressure tactics designed to stop you from thinking carefully. Legitimate opportunities don't evaporate in 24 hours.

**Unrealistic claims about the product.** If a brand is asking you to make claims that seem exaggerated or that you can't verify — health claims, performance guarantees — be very careful. Promoting misleading claims can expose you to reputational and legal risk.

**Requests for upfront payment.** Any arrangement that asks you to pay for the product first and "recoup" it through commission or a future fee structure is almost always a scam or a very bad deal.

**Excessive usage rights for low compensation.** If a brand is asking for exclusivity, ad usage rights, and whitelisting for a $50 product or a small flat fee, the value exchange is fundamentally skewed against you.
